Sample efficiency comes from the function class, not the neuron basis

What the statistical-learning, superposition, and modularity literatures — and the program’s own studies — say about structural vs functional modularity.

TL;DR. The program’s lever is to fit functions with a sparse computation graph more sample-efficiently than \(\ell_1\)/\(\ell_2\). The question this note settles from theory and from our own experiments: does aligning the network’s realization to the neuron basis (structural modularity) buy efficiency over confining the function class to the right sparse structure (functional modularity), and is the gap between the two caused by nonlinearity? The verified answer is no and yes. Every formal handle on sample efficiency — nonparametric minimax rates and norm-based generalization bounds — is a property of the function class and is invariant to rotation of the representation basis (\eqref{eq:rate}–\eqref{eq:rad}); no theorem rewards one-neuron-per-feature. Nonlinearity is what severs structural from functional: it manufactures non-axis-aligned mixed selectivity, so the loss-optimal code is a rotated, superposed functional decomposition — the natural product of training (and why LLMs are polysemantic and still work). Empirically, enforced block modularity is inert (Mittal 2022; Csordás 2021), and our emergent modularity study supplies the sharp version: the most structurally modular prior we have ties plain \(\ell_1\) to within 1% and buys zero compositional generalization, while confinement to the correct graph wins 14.5×. The prize is confinement to the right (possibly rotated) structure, not neuron alignment. One unsettled caveat: structural alignment may help in the heavy-noise regime (§7).

1. The question

Modern networks place features in superposition: one neuron participates in many features and one feature spreads across many neurons. This is the documented, loss-driven behaviour of large models, and it works. So a modular decomposition realized in a rotated, many-neuron basis — functional modularity — appears to be the natural product of training. Against that backdrop, a per-neuron representation prior that pushes the weights toward a block-diagonal, one-neuron-per-feature layout looks like it might be fighting the optimizer for no reward. This note asks whether it is.

Three things get conflated under the word “modularity,” and the whole argument turns on separating them. The target object throughout is a sparse computation graph: \(f^\star\) is computed by a DAG of intermediate nodes arranged in \(\Delta\) tiers, each node a nonlinear map of at most \(r\) parents (per-node fan-in \(r \ll d\)) — a hierarchy, not a single layer. The additive form \(f^\star(x)=\sum_k g_k(x_{S_k})\) with small \(S_k\) is only its depth-one (\(\Delta{=}1\)) special case.

Functional modularity — a property of what is computed: \(f^\star\) factors through that sparse computation graph (low per-node fan-in, possibly deep). The network may realize the graph in rotated, superposed coordinates that mix many neurons; functional modularity does not require neuron alignment.
Structural modularity — a property of the trained realization: the same low-fan-in graph, but routed in the neuron basis — block-diagonal weights, one-neuron-per-feature alignment. Structural is functional plus axis-alignment.
Confinement — a property of the search: the prior or architecture restricts the hypothesis class to the correct graph throughout training, not merely at the endpoint.

Graph-sparse is not input-sparse. A deep fan-in-\(r\) graph composes nonlinear nodes, so it can carry high input-interaction order (high Sobol \(\mathrm{F}\)) while remaining graph-sparse — the root of a depth-\(\Delta\) tree reads up to \(r^{\Delta}\) inputs with high-order interactions among them. So “functional sparsity” in the narrow sense of a low-order additive map (Sobol \(\mathrm{F}\approx0\)) is a distinct, weaker property, and — per emergent modularity — is not the efficiency source: confinement to the graph is. The additive special case is not the object of interest.

Sharpened, the question is: does aligning the realization to the neuron basis (structural) buy anything beyond confining the function class to the correct sparse computation graph (functional)? The remainder argues the answer is no — sample efficiency is a confinement phenomenon that is blind to basis (§3) — and that nonlinearity is precisely why the correct functional structure is generically not axis-aligned (§4), so a structural prior reaches for an object the optimum does not put on the axes (§6).

3. Sample efficiency is a function-class property

Every rigorous account of “structure buys sample efficiency” locates the gain in a restriction of the function class, and none of them mentions the neuron basis.

The fan-in sets the exponent, not the ambient dimension — but not by a single substitution. For an \(s\)-smooth function of all \(d\) inputs the optimal minimax (squared-error) rate is \(n^{-2s/(2s+d)}\) (Stone 1982, 3–0): reaching error \(\varepsilon\) costs \(n\sim\varepsilon^{-(2s+d)/2s}\), exponential in \(d\) — the curse. Two structural restrictions break it, and they are not the same object.

(i) Input-level (shallow) sparsity. If \(f^\star\) is a sum of terms each reading at most \(r\) of the inputs — an additive or order-\(r\) interaction model — the ambient \(d\) is replaced by the interaction order \(r\) (Stone 1985, 1994):

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:rate} \underbrace{n^{-\,2s/(2s+r)}}_{\text{interaction order } r} \;\;\gg\;\; \underbrace{n^{-\,2s/(2s+d)}}_{\text{ambient } d}, \qquad r \ll d . \end{equation}\]

(ii) Hierarchical (deep) sparsity. A deep computation graph is generally not input-sparse — its root reads up to \(r^{\Delta}\) inputs at high interaction order — so \eqref{eq:rate} does not hold with \(r\) read as the per-node fan-in. The deep case is governed by composition instead (Schmidt-Hieber 2020, 3–0):

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:sh} \phi_n \;=\; \max_{i=0,\dots,q}\; n^{-\,2\beta_i^\ast/(2\beta_i^\ast + t_i)}, \qquad \beta_i^\ast=\beta_i\!\!\prod_{l\gt i}\min(\beta_l,1). \end{equation}\]

The fan-in here is the per-node fan-in, binding intermediate nodes, not just inputs — exactly what \eqref{eq:sh} encodes: \(t_i\) is the number of parents a tier-\(i\) node reads (\(\beta_i\) its smoothness), so the dimension in each factor is the small fan-in, never \(d\) — the reduction does survive depth. Two refinements the shallow form \eqref{eq:rate} hides: the rate is the max over tiers (the bottleneck tier dominates, not one clean exponent), and the smoothness is the composition-degraded effective smoothness \(\beta_i^\ast\le s\), not \(s\). So the headline — fan-in, not ambient \(d\), governs the rate — holds for deep graphs (the program’s own theory note argues the same), while the clean \(n^{-2s/(2s+r)}\) of \eqref{eq:rate} is the shallow idealization the deep rate refines. Bauer & Kohler (2019, 3–0) reach the same fan-in-not-\(d\) conclusion for hierarchical models. Nothing in these rates references the neuron basis.

Sparse recovery: irrelevant variables are nearly free once you confine to the support. Lasso support recovery has a sharp sample-complexity threshold (Wainwright 2006/2009, 3–0 for the threshold fact):

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:lasso} n \;\gt\; 2(\theta_u+\epsilon)\,\log(p-k) \;+\; k \;+\; 1 , \end{equation}\]

ambient \(p\) entering only logarithmically, sparsity \(k\) linearly. (The stronger reading — that confinement converts the cost from polynomial to logarithmic in general — was killed 1–2: the Wainwright converse is Lasso failure, not a limit over all estimators. Quote \eqref{eq:lasso}, not the sweeping interpretation.)

The load-bearing fact: generalization bounds are rotation-invariant. For norm-bounded networks the empirical Rademacher complexity is (Golowich, Rakhlin & Shamir 2018, 3–0)

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:rad} \widehat{\mathfrak{R}}(\mathcal{F}) \;\le\; \frac{B\bigl(\sqrt{2\log 2\,\cdot\,\mathrm{depth}}+1\bigr)\,\prod_j M_F(j)}{\sqrt{m}}, \qquad \lVert QW\rVert_F=\lVert W\rVert_F , \end{equation}\]

where \(M_F(j)\) bounds the Frobenius norm of layer \(j\): only \(\sqrt{\mathrm{depth}}\) dependence, no width or parameter-count term. Because the Frobenius norm is unitarily invariant, an axis-aligned (structural) and a rotated/superposed (functional) solution with equal weight norms receive an identical bound. Neuron-basis alignment is not a quantity this theory can see; weight magnitude is. The caveat. Equations \eqref{eq:rate}–\eqref{eq:sh} are existence/minimax results — a good estimator exists and the rate is intrinsic — not a guarantee that SGD finds it; and norm bounds like \eqref{eq:rad} can be loose in practice (Nagarajan & Kolter 2019). The residual role for any prior is therefore as an optimization bias toward the confined region — decided by experiment (§6), not by these theorems.

4. Why structural ≠ functional: nonlinearity and a symmetry of the loss

Two mechanisms compound. The objective is rotation-invariant, so the optimum is a whole rotation-equivalence class and gradient descent has no force selecting the axis-aligned member: the minimax rate is a function-class property (§3), the capacity term is unitarily invariant \eqref{eq:rad}, and the loss-optimal superposition embedding is itself defined only up to rotation (§5). Nonlinearity manufactures non-axis-aligned codes. Neurons with nonlinear mixed selectivity — responses not expressible as a linear sum of single-variable tunings — yield high-dimensional population codes from which one linear readout realizes exponentially many functions, \(N_c=2^d\) (Rigotti et al. 2013, 3–0); pure and even linear-mixed selectivity can only generate low-dimensional codes (2–1). Nonlinearity is the thing that pushes the code off the neuron axes.

The keystone. Under biological constraints — nonnegativity and energy efficiency in both activity and weights — optimal single neurons align to the axes of variation, but the theory “explain[s] when the brain instead entangles representations in response to entangled task factors” (Whittington, Dorrell, Ganguli & Behrens 2023, ICLR). Distilled: alignment is optimal in the near-linear, separably-mapped regime; an entangling (nonlinear/multiplicative) map makes persistent mixed selectivity optimal and the code unalignable to single neurons. This is the cleanest formal statement that nonlinearity breaks the structural↔functional equivalence — but it was not independently re-verified in this pass and the abstract phrases the condition as “entangled task factors,” not literally “iff linear,” so its exact hypotheses should be checked before it carries manuscript weight.

Citation note. The keystone is ICLR 2023 (arXiv:2210.01768), authors Whittington, Dorrell, Ganguli & Behrens — not a Nature paper, and with no coauthor “Barry.” That misattribution is a common slip from this group’s separate Tolman–Eichenbaum-Machine grid-cell lineage (Cell 2020 / Nature, where Caswell Barry is a coauthor); the theory note’s Prop 3 already cites it correctly.

The program’s own theory note reaches the same conclusion internally and more sharply: a penalty-only per-neuron code receives no task gradient (Prop 1), collapses to a constant (Prop 2), and — the localizability ceiling (Prop 3) — can express only axis-aligned structure, so it is blind to a module that occupies a rotated subspace even when forward-coupled. Its forensics show leading-direction concentration \(0.3\)–\(0.7\) (not \(1\)) and neuron-partition \(\mathrm{ARI}\approx 0\) even where the regularizer performs best. The external literature and the program’s theory are the same argument.

5. Superposition is the loss-optimal natural product

The premise that superposed functional modularity is what training naturally produces is well-supported. ReLU networks on sparse inputs “represent more features than they have dimensions” by placing them on near-orthogonal directions — a capacity benefit at the price of interference, with sparsity causally driving the network to push features off the neuron axes (Elhage et al. 2022, 3–0; indexed by the harness at arXiv:2209.10652). Optimal capacity allocation makes the most important features monosemantic and the rest polysemantic in proportion to loss impact, so polysemanticity is the loss-optimal solution, and the efficient embedding factorizes

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:qbp} W \;=\; Q\,B\,P \qquad (Q\ \text{orthogonal},\ B\ \text{block-diagonal},\ P\ \text{permutation}), \end{equation}\]

invariant to rotation, with block size set by architecture (Scherlis et al. 2022; allocation 3–0, the \(W=QBP\) form 2–1). Whether neurons look monosemantic is an architecture-dependent byproduct, not what the loss rewards.

But expressivity is not sample efficiency. The \(2^d\) result is flexibility, not generalization; the same line of work shows high-dimensional mixed selectivity gives high discriminability but low generalization, while low-dimensional, factorized/abstract codes generalize better (Bernardi et al. 2020). So on the structural-vs-functional axis, “functional” must mean the correct low-dimensional factorization (which may be rotated), not “more superposition is better.”

6. The empirical record: structural modularity is inert

External. On a benchmark with known ground-truth modular structure, modular systems beat monolithic ones “often only by a small margin”; the oracle with perfect ground-truth specialization wins, “especially in the regime of many rules,” while end-to-end training “do[es] not discover perfect specialization” and collapse “becomes worse with increasing number of rules” — the titular answer is no (Mittal, Bengio & Lajoie 2022, 3–0). Common networks also fail to reuse/recombine submodules, tied to poor systematic generalization (Csordás et al. 2021, 3–0). The prize is correct specialization plus extra inductive bias, not block structure per se.

7. The counter-current and the honest caveats

The dichotomy is not closed against structural alignment everywhere. A recent result claims enforced monosemantic features beat polysemantic ones under heavy corruption (e.g. +13.7% top-1 on ImageNet-100 at 90% label noise), reversing in the clean regime (arXiv:2410.21331). It failed this pass’s verification (1–2), so it is unsettled, not refuted — and it points at a genuine, regime-dependent tension in exactly the sample-scarce regime the program cares about. Robustness-under-noise is a different axis from the clean-label minimax rate of §3. Combined with the ERM≠SGD gap (§3), the live empirical question is which prior best steers SGD into the confined region under realistic noise — where, on present evidence, structural priors (Lf, axdn) lose to \(\ell_1\) while correct-functional confinement (Fc) wins.

8. Verdict and implications

Does structural modularity have a sample-efficiency benefit over functional? No — not from neuron-basis alignment as such. Efficiency is a function-class property (\eqref{eq:rate}–\eqref{eq:rad}), bought by confining to the correct sparse structure, which is rotation-agnostic; the most-structural prior ties \(\ell_1\) and a neuron-aligned wiring prior loses to it, while the functional oracle wins 14.5×.

Do they fail to be equivalent because of nonlinearity? Yes. The loss is rotation-invariant, so the optimum is a rotation class with no axis-aligned preference; and nonlinearity produces mixed selectivity that is alignable to neurons only in the near-linear regime. With a nonlinear factor-to-representation map the correct functional modules occupy rotated subspaces a per-neuron code provably cannot express. In the linear case the two coincide; nonlinearity is what severs them.

For the program. Stop treating neuron-basis alignment as the goal: it is not a source of sample efficiency, and our own results confirm it ties or loses to \(\ell_1\). The lever is confinement to the correct, rotation-agnostic structure — Fc’s 14.5× is the ceiling. The open problem this isolates is a learned cost that self-discovers the right structure (not a sparser or randomly-modular one), which by Prop 1 requires the structure-defining object to sit in the task-gradient path. That is why explicit communication’s forward-coupled de-rotation is the most promising thread — though its edge is currently alignment, not accuracy — and why the single most decisive next experiment is the noise×scarcity crossover that would either rescue or bury the structural hypothesis on its last defensible ground (§7); its plan is drafted under studies/explicit_communication/plan/.
