Does the communication matrix carry module structure?

Can a measured score \(A\) beat the \(\lvert W\rvert\) proxy it would replace?

Learned representations & modularitywipbuilds on: What is the Separator regularizer actually doing?, The flat winning ticket under the microscopereproduce: 2fcdfa8 · feature/runstore-pipeline

TL;DR. Before building a regularizer that prices each connection by a measured neuron-to-neuron communication score \(A_{ij}\) (replacing the static weight-magnitude proxy \(\lvert W_{ij}\rvert\)), we pre-registered a gate testing whether \(A\) actually carries module structure. On planted nets, \(A\) recovers disjoint modules perfectly (H1 met, ARI \(=1.0\)) and — the decisive test — does not transitively merge two pipelined modules across a strong reusability bottleneck (H2 pass: \(G{+}1\) communities recovered, root never absorbed in 10/10 seeds). But \(A\) never beats \(\lvert W\rvert\) (H3 fail: pooled \(\Delta\text{ARI}=-0.01\), not significant) — and in the realistic regime the modules are rotated, not neuron-aligned (H4 branch (b) on both tasks). Decision: do not build the \(A\)-based regularizer as a replacement for \(\lvert W\rvert\); the blocking hypothesis is H3. The communication matrix is real and well-behaved, but it furnishes no advantage over the proxy already in use.

1. Motivation

The problem. In the current NeuRep objective the per-neuron codes \(\phi_i\) receive no task gradient, and each connection is priced by its weight magnitude \(\rho(W_{ij})\) — a static, data-free proxy for how much two neurons belong together. The companion forensics (learned codes don't make weights modular, the flat winning ticket under the microscope) show the consequence: the codes collapse or act only as a capacity-pruning bias, and the task's real modules sit in rotated subspaces that mix many neurons.

The proposed fix. Stop pricing connections by weight magnitude; instead shape the codes with a measured, data-driven communication score \(A_{ij}\). The contrastive module-detection term \(\mathcal{R}_{\text{mod}}\) pulls two neurons' codes together when their communication \(A_{ij}\) is strong and pushes them apart — up to a margin \(m\) — when it is weak. The intuition: neurons that consistently participate in the same computation should end up with aligned representations \(\phi\). Concretely,

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:mod} \mathcal{R}_{\text{mod}} = \sum_{\ell}\sum_{i,j}\Big[\,\widetilde A^{(\ell)}_{ij}\,d_{ij} \;+\;\big(1-\widetilde A^{(\ell)}_{ij}\big)\,\max\!\big(0,\,m-d_{ij}\big)\Big], \qquad d_{ij}=\lVert\phi_i-\phi_j\rVert . \end{equation}\]

Here \(d_{ij}=\lVert\phi_i-\phi_j\rVert\) is the distance between the two codes and \(\widetilde A^{(\ell)}_{ij}\in[0,1]\) the detached, within-layer-normalized communication score. The first term attracts strongly-communicating pairs; the second repels weakly-communicating pairs until they are at least the margin \(m\) apart, so the trivial collapse (all codes equal, \(d_{ij}\equiv0\)) leaves the repulsion positive and is no longer optimal — modules should fall out as clusters of mutually-close codes.

The risk. Strong communication is not the same as shared module membership: in a hierarchy one module consumes the output of another, so a hierarchical task must contain strong inter-module (pipeline) edges. The repulsion in \eqref{eq:mod} is weighted by \((1-\widetilde A_{ij})\), so it puts no separating force on the high-\(A\) interface edges between two pipelined modules. Whether they stay separable then depends entirely on whether \(A\) has community structure — dense within a module, sparse between. If \(A\) lacks that structure, no \(A\)-based regularizer can recover the hierarchy. Hence we validate \(A\) first.

2. Measuring communication

To price connections by communication we must first measure it. On each trained net we compute three candidate scores from the effective weights and the in-distribution activations \(a^{(\ell)}\) (sender \(j\) post-activation, receiver \(i\)):

\[\begin{align} \textbf{(M1)}\ & A^{(\ell)}_{ij}=\mathbb{E}_x\big|\,W^{(\ell)}_{ij}\,a^{(\ell)}_j\,\big|, \label{eq:m1}\\ \textbf{(M2)}\ & A^{(\ell)}_{ij}=\big|\mathrm{corr}_x\!\big(a^{(\ell)}_j,\,a^{(\ell+1)}_i\big)\big|, \label{eq:m2}\\ \textbf{(M3)}\ & A^{(\ell)}_{ij}=\mathbb{E}_x\Big|\,\tfrac{\partial \hat y}{\partial z^{(\ell+1)}_i}\cdot W^{(\ell)}_{ij}\cdot a^{(\ell)}_j\,\Big|. \label{eq:m3} \end{align}\]

M1 is raw activation-weighted flow; M2 is how correlated a sender and receiver fire; M3 weights each edge by how much the network's output actually depends on it, using the output Jacobian \(\partial\hat y/\partial z_i\) (not the loss gradient, which vanishes at convergence). Two controls bracket every score: \(A=\lvert W\rvert\) (the current proxy — the bar to beat) and a per-layer shuffle (negative control); a non-gating ceiling sets \(A=\) the planted 0/1 mask, confirming the detector works when \(A\) is perfect.

3. Experimental design

Reproducibility (R2). Replication: 10 seeds per setting; each seed redraws the random teachers (S1/S2) or the task instance + init (S3), so results are over the random task family, not one instance. Training: all nets GELU + AdamW (weight-decay 0), learning rate \(3\times10^{-3}\), batch 200, eval every 500 steps, patience 20, best-validation-checkpoint selection, cap 6000 steps (the nets are tiny and converge by ~2–3k, so the chosen checkpoint is unaffected by the cap). Fit gates (a net's scores are read only if it fit): planted test \(R^2\!\ge\!0.90\); S3 first-order Sobol sum \(\ge\!0.30\). All 10/10 planted seeds and 20/20 S3 records passed (S1 \(R^2{=}0.998\), S2 \(R^2{=}0.970\), S3 Sobol \(0.96\!-\!0.99\)). Statistics: bars are the mean over seeds with per-seed points overlaid; error bars on \(\Delta\)ARI are \(\pm\)1 s.e.m.; H3 uses the paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test with Cohen's \(d_z\). Code: apparatus validated by 10 unit tests (masks isolate modules; the score reader equals model(x); the M3 Jacobian equals M1 on the output layer; the planted-mask ceiling recovers blocks; shuffling collapses recovery).

3.1 Settings

Two planted masked MLPs, where the module of every neuron and every inter-module edge is known by construction, plus the realistic net:

  • S1 — disjoint modules (necessary condition). \(G{=}4\) independent sub-nets on disjoint inputs, \(G\) separate outputs, no inter-module edges anywhere. Ground truth: the \(G\) blocks.
  • S2 — hierarchy with a reusability bottleneck (decisive). \(G\) leaf sub-nets each emit one scalar; those scalars feed a shared root sub-net. Masking makes the only inter-module edges the leaf-scalar\(\to\)root-input ones. Ground truth: \(\{\text{leaf}_1,\dots,\text{leaf}_G,\text{root}\}\) (\(G{+}1\) modules) with a known bottleneck. The S1\(\to\)S2 contrast isolates exactly the transitive-merging risk above.
  • S3 — realistic. The standard \(24\!\to\!64\!\to\!64\!\to\!1\) GELU MLP, plain training, on flat_modular (w=4, ν=16) and hier_tree (w=4, d=3, ν=16). No hidden ground truth; input neurons carry their factor-group labels, and we add three rotation-aware subspace probes (defined in §3.2; adapted from flat_forensics).

3.2 Detection and metrics

We stack the per-layer \(\widetilde A\) (within-layer max-normalized) into one symmetrized neuron graph and detect communities: known-\(k\) spectral for S1/S2 (isolating "does \(A\) carry the signal" from "can clustering find \(k\)") and modularity (Louvain, chooses \(k\)) for S3. Recovery is scored by the Adjusted Rand Index (ARI; \(1\) = perfect agreement with the ground-truth partition, \(0\) = chance) and NMI; we also report the recovered community count, the block contrast \(B(A)=\overline{A}_{\text{within}}/\overline{A}_{\text{between}}\) (mean within-module over mean between-module edge weight; \(\ge1\) means dense-within / sparse-between), the S2 merge indicator (\(1\) if the root is absorbed into a leaf community, \(0\) if it keeps its own), and, for S3, the input-group ARI (do the input neurons cluster by their factor group?).

For S3 we add three rotation-aware subspace probes that test whether a module lives in a rotated hidden subspace rather than on individual neurons: axis_mass (mean squared largest single-neuron loading of a module's estimated hidden subspace — \(1\) = neuron-aligned, \(\approx\!1/\text{width}\) = fully spread), patch_r2 (the causal \(R^2\) with which patching only a module's rotated subspace reproduces the teacher's output response to resampling that module's inputs — high means the rotated subspace carries the module), and lane_diag (the fraction of a module's layer-1 subspace energy that flows into its own layer-2 subspace).

4. The validation gate

The gate poses two questions. A capability question — can a communication score see modules at all, and keep two pipelined ones apart? (H1, H2.) And an advantage question — does a measured score actually beat the \(\lvert W\rvert\) proxy we already use, and are per-neuron codes even the right object? (H3, H4.) Every threshold was fixed in advance.

Decision rule. Build \(\mathcal{R}_{\text{mod}}\) (\eqref{eq:mod}) only if some single score satisfies H1 and H2 and H3; H4 then sets the representation class. H1–H3 are confirmatory and gating; H4 is non-gating.

H3 is the kill switch. It is the blocking hypothesis: if a measured \(A\) fails to outperform the \(\lvert W\rvert\) proxy by a clear margin, there is no reason to replace the proxy and the redesign is effectively closed — however well H1 and H2 go.

4.1 Capability H1, H2 pass

H1 — disjoint modules (S1). Every measured score recovers the planted modules perfectly — ARI \(=1.000\), NMI \(=1.000\) — far above the shuffle control (ARI \(=0.013\)) and matching the planted-mask ceiling (Fig. 1, left). The necessary condition is comfortably met; one caveat is already visible — \(\lvert W\rvert\) is also perfect here, a foreshadowing of H3.

H2 — the reusability bottleneck (S2). The decisive test, and the score passes it. Across the leaf\(\to\)root bottleneck the scores recover modules with ARI \(0.88\!-\!0.91\) (well above the \(0.60\) bar); the choose-\(k\) detector finds exactly \(G{+}1=5\) communities in 10/10 seeds (M1, M2); and the root keeps its own community in 10/10 seeds for every score (merge indicator \(=0\)) — it is never absorbed into a leaf. The feared transitive merging simply does not happen: within-module density (block contrast \(B\approx6\!-\!9\)) dominates the single high-\(A\) interface, so detection holds the root apart even though the contrastive term, alone, would not (Figs. 1–2).

4.2 Advantage H3 fail

H3 — value over the \(\lvert W\rvert\) proxy. The kill switch fires: no measured score beats \(\lvert W\rvert\). Pooling S1+S2, the paired advantage is slightly negative and not significant for every score:

The planted-net result is partly confounded: masking makes \(\lvert W\rvert\) equal the ground-truth wiring by construction, so it is a near-perfect proxy there and nothing can beat it. The fair test is the realistic regime (S3, input-group ARI): there the best measured score (M2) does edge ahead of \(\lvert W\rvert\) — by \(+0.163\) on flat_modular and \(+0.056\) on hier_tree — but neither gap is significant (\(p=0.105,\,0.68\)) and both absolute ARIs sit well below \(0.50\). Either way, the measured communication score delivers no decisive advantage over the weight-magnitude proxy already in use.

H4 — representation class (non-gating). On both S3 tasks the input-group ARI is weak and high-variance (best score: 0.36 flat, 0.24 hier; both below the \(0.50\) branch-(a) bar), while the rotation-aware patch_r2 reliably recovers the modules from rotated subspaces (0.92 flat; 0.47 mean / 0.59 median hier) far above the neuron-level view and the rotation-blind control, with low axis_mass (\(\approx0.23\), i.e. not single-neuron-aligned). Both tasks therefore land in branch (b): structure is real but rotated — per-neuron codes are the wrong object; only rotation-aware codes could carry it. This corroborates the flat-forensics rotated-modules finding. H4 is non-gating (it would set the representation class only if the gate passed).

5. Verdict

Decision (rule of §4: proceed iff some single score satisfies H1 ∧ H2 ∧ H3). H1 met, H2 pass, H3 fail ⇒ do not proceed to the \(A\)-based regularizer. Blocking hypothesis: H3. Two findings are genuinely encouraging for the concept — a measured \(A\) does carry module structure (H1) and does not transitively merge pipelined modules (H2, the risk that motivated the gate). But the premise of the redesign — that a measured communication score beats the static \(\lvert W\rvert\) proxy — is unsupported: across planted and realistic regimes, M1/M2/M3 tie or marginally trail \(\lvert W\rvert\). And H4 indicates the modules are rotated, so per-neuron codes are likely the wrong object regardless.

The action fixed in advance for an H3 failure is to reconsider the premise. The constructive reading: if a code regularizer is pursued, the lever is the representation class (rotation-aware codes, H4 branch b), not swapping \(\lvert W\rvert\) for a measured \(A\) — that swap is not justified by this evidence.

6. Caveats

  • Confound (flagged, not hidden): on planted nets \(\lvert W\rvert\) equals the ground-truth mask by construction, so H3-on-planted is biased toward \(\lvert W\rvert\). The S3 supplement is the fair test and still shows no significant advantage.
  • Directional prediction not supported (non-gating): the plan predicted \(M3>M2>M1\) and that M2 (co-activation) would show the most S2 merging. We see M1\(\approx\)M2 \(\approx\)M3 on S2 ARI, zero merging for all scores, and the highest block contrast for M3 — the ordering and the merging prediction do not hold.
  • Scope: planted nets are small (\(\le\)~120 neurons) with smooth tanh teachers; hier_tree (d=3) is the hardest to fit (higher MSE) and its patch_r2 control is heavy-tailed (we report the median). The gate's decision rests on H3, which is robust across both planted settings and the realistic supplement.
