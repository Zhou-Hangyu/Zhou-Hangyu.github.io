When does a neuron representation make a network modular?

A gradient-path, degeneracy, and localizability account — and why our learned codes don’t

Encoding & modularity theorywipbuilds on: An Exact MLP Encoding of Computation Graphsreproduce: 3394642 · feature/runstore-pipeline

TL;DR. A large literature shows that embedding neurons in a space and pricing weights by distance turns a network modular (BIMT, seRNN, Jacobs–Jordan, TDANN, NeuroPos, Space-as-Time). Our learned per-neuron representations (Separator, WiringPrior, axon–dendrite) do not: their geometry collapses and never aligns with the task’s factors. We explain the gap with three facts. (P1) Decoupling: a representation that only prices weights gets no task gradient (\(\partial\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{task}}/\partial\pi\equiv0\)), so its geometry is the fixed point of regularizer \(\times\) optimizer, not of the task. (P2) Degeneracy: a free representation under a symmetric distance has a collapse minimum (\(\pi\!\equiv\!\mathrm{const}\Rightarrow\mathcal{R}{=}0\)); fixity, anchoring, a description-length term, or a forward benefit of distance are the only escapes. (P3) Localizability: a per-neuron code can express only axis-aligned (up-to-permutation) module structure, and by a theorem of Whittington et al. axis-aligned modules are the energy optimum only when the factor-to-code map is linear — under nonlinear computation the modules sit in rotated subspaces that no per-neuron code can name. The successful methods all break exactly one of P1–P3 (forward-coupling, fixed/anchored geometry, or a discrete swap); the free penalty-only quadrant our methods occupy breaks none, so it can price weights but never discover the task’s decomposition. The companion reports test each prediction.

The representation-coupled objective

A neuron-representation method augments a network of weights \(W\) with a per-neuron representation \(\pi=\{\pi_i\}\) (positions in \(\mathbb R^d\), membership codes in \([0,1]^K\), grid slots) and a wiring penalty that makes weight between “far” neurons expensive: \[\begin{equation} \label{eq:obj} \min_{W,\pi}\ \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{task}}(W)\;+\;\lambda\sum_{\ell}\sum_{i,j}\,c(\pi_i,\pi_j)\,\rho\!\big(W^{(\ell)}_{ij}\big), \qquad \mathcal{R}(W,\pi):=\sum_{\ell,i,j}c(\pi_i,\pi_j)\,\rho(W^{(\ell)}_{ij}). \end{equation}\] This is the common form of BIMT (\(c=\) grid \(\ell_1\) distance), seRNN (\(c=\) Euclidean\(\times\)communicability), Jacobs–Jordan (\(c=\|r_i-r_j\|^{10}\)), WiringPrior and Separator. The hope is that minimizing \(\mathcal{R}\) drags functionally-related neurons together into modules whose weight blocks match the task’s functional decomposition \(f=\sum_k f_k(z_{S_k})\) (or its DAG). We ask when this can succeed.

One distinction organizes everything.

Call \(\pi\) forward-coupled if the network’s output depends on it, so \(\partial\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{task}}/\partial\pi\not\equiv0\) (NeuroPos: \(w_{ij}=1/\|p_i-p_j\|\) is the weight; Space-as-Time: \(p\) sets a synaptic delay), and penalty-only if \(\pi\) enters only through \(\mathcal{R}\) (seRNN, BIMT, WiringPrior, Separator: predictions never read \(\pi\)). The two behave completely differently.

P1 — Decoupling: a penalty-only representation never sees the task

Proposition 1 (Decoupling). If \(\pi\) is penalty-only then \(\partial\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{task}}/\partial\pi\equiv0\), so under gradient descent the only forces on \(\pi\) are \(\lambda\,\nabla_{\pi}\mathcal{R}(W(t),\pi)\) and weight decay. Hence the stationary geometry of \(\pi\) is a fixed point of the regularizer and the optimizer evaluated at the current weights \(W(t)\); the task influences \(\pi\) only through the slow shadow it casts on \(W(t)\).

The proof is one line of the chain rule, but the consequence is strong and we have verified it directly: replaying the recorded weight trajectory \(W(t)\) through the regularizer’s own code, from the run’s initial \(\pi\), reproduces the live geometry to three digits (peak packing step \(900\) vs \(900\), silhouette \(0.806\) vs \(0.807\)) — nothing the task does reaches the geometry except through \(W\). The same point is made from the outside by Csordás et al.: in a standard network “routing can only occur through the weights … [which] would change the data representation alongside the routing unless the weights have a special structure that we empirically find is hard to learn.” Mészáros et al. make it sharpest, deliberately withholding the penalty gradient from their positions “so that only the topology … and not the geometry” is shaped, and predicting that penalty-only positions “would converge toward the centre, [and] the benefits of modularity … would vanish.” This is the regime our methods live in.

What can \(\pi\) read from the \(W\)-shadow? Only what a per-neuron statistic of \(W\) exposes — and we show in P3 that for the modular tasks of interest this shadow is rotated, so it carries no axis-aligned signal a per-neuron code could lock onto. The result is that \(\pi\) relaxes to a geometry set by the optimizer: Adam drives the unbounded position field to the \(2^d\) sign-orthant corners of \(\mathbb R^d\) and the bounded code field to the \(2^K\) corners of \([0,1]^K\), in both cases an artifact of the update rule, not the task.

P2 — Degeneracy: a free representation collapses

Proposition 2 (Collapse). If \(c\) is a symmetric pseudometric with \(c(\pi,\pi)=0\) and the \(\pi_i\) are free parameters, then \(\pi_i\!\equiv\!\mathrm{const}\) gives \(\mathcal{R}(W,\pi)=0\) for every \(W\), so it is a global minimizer of the penalty. Penalty-only \(+\) free \(\Rightarrow\) a collapse attractor.

Empirically our codes do collapse: on the hierarchical cell \(100\%\) of cross-boundary code pairs reach distance \(<10^{-3}\), and under a symmetry-broken init the collapse is merely delayed (an annealed curriculum) while a uniform init is “born collapsed” and never recovers. The escapes are exactly the design choices of the working methods: (a) fix \(\pi\) (seRNN, Jacobs–Jordan, TDANN, ITN place neurons on an immovable grid — “space … imposes a single fixed configuration”); (b) anchor a subset of codes to known identities (our MLP-encoding note’s refined code; companion Report B); (c) add a description-length or uniformity term that makes collapse the unique maximum of a counter-penalty; or (d) give distance a forward benefit so spreading lowers \(\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{task}}\) (Space-as-Time, NeuroPos). Anti-collapse alone is necessary but not sufficient: forcing codes apart with a uniformity term prevents collapse yet leaves anatomical modularity \(Q\) decoupled from function — the most modular run we produced this way was the worst performing.

P3 — Localizability: a per-neuron code can only name axis-aligned modules

A per-neuron representation assigns each neuron a single point/code; the structure it can express is therefore a (soft) partition of neurons — an axis-aligned module assignment, defined only up to the permutation symmetry of a fully-connected layer (“because neurons … have permutation symmetries, there is no incentive for them to develop anatomical modularity” — Liu et al.). It cannot represent a module that occupies a rotated subspace, i.e. a linear combination of many neurons. Whether the network’s modules are axis-aligned or rotated is therefore decisive, and it is not a free choice:

Proposition 3 (Localizability ceiling, after Whittington et al. 2023). Under non-negativity and energy (activity/weight-norm) minimization, the optimal representation of independent factors is axis-aligned (each neuron selective for one factor) if and only if the factor-to-representation map is linear. When the map is nonlinear, mixed selectivity persists and de-mixes only as layers approach the output; genuinely entangled tasks are provably unalignable.

So the rotation is intrinsic to the computation. Our synthetic leaves \(f_k\) are nonlinear (sinusoids), which by Proposition 3 forces the hidden modules into rotated subspaces — and indeed a forensic probe finds each module spread across many neurons (mean leading-direction concentration \(0.3\)–\(0.7\), not \(1\)) with near-zero neuron-partition agreement (ARI \(\approx0\)) even where the regularizer performs best. No per-neuron code, with or without a task gradient, can name such a module; unsupervised identifiability is impossible without an inductive bias because any rotation of the latent is an equally good model (Locatello et al.). P3 is thus a ceiling that sits above P1–P2: even a forward-coupled, non-collapsing code is blind to a rotated decomposition.

The taxonomy: three escapes, and the one quadrant that has none

Place every method on two axes — is \(\pi\) in the gradient path? and is \(\pi\) free to collapse? (Table 1). Every method that demonstrably induces task-aligned modularity escapes via one of P1–P2: a fixed informative geometry (seRNN’s goal/choice spatial split; Jacobs–Jordan’s what/where), forward-coupling (NeuroPos, Space-as-Time, whose authors report monotonically rising modularity \(Q\)), or a discrete swap that reassigns neurons to fixed slots (BIMT, Growing Brains — where plain \(\ell_1\) produces functional clusters that sit inside a shuffle null, and only the swap pushes them out to anatomical modularity). The free-learned-penalty-only quadrant — the one our Separator, WiringPrior, and axon–dendrite methods occupy — escapes none, and is the only quadrant for which no published method demonstrates task-alignment. It can price weights (and so post a test-loss gain, as a capacity governor) but cannot discover structure.

Predictions (tested in the companion reports).

(1) Free penalty-only codes collapse and yield less task-aligned input-block weight modularity than a plain \(\ell_1\) magnitude penalty, at any coupling strength (Report 2). (2) A fixed informative prior recovers block structure though it too is penalty-only — fixity, not gradient, is what matters — provided the coupling is strong enough. (3) The achievable alignment is capped by localizability: on a neuron-localizable (discrete) task even modest methods align, while on the nonlinear/rotated task no method, including a fixed oracle, reaches it — the rotation barrier of Proposition 3 (Report 3). (4) Putting the same per-neuron geometry in the forward pass (NeuroPos) keeps it engaged where its penalty-only twin (WiringPrior) collapses. Together these say precisely under what conditions a neuron representation can make a network modular — and why ours, as built, cannot.
