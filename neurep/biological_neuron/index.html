Local fan-in selectivity across species

A neuron contacts a small, cell-type-structured fraction of the partners it could reach nearby — and the fraction you measure depends entirely on the denominator you choose.

Referencereferencereproduce: f3169e1 · master

Summary. A cortical neuron carries thousands of synapses yet connects to only a minority of the neurons it could reach locally. Measured as a local connection probability — connected pairs divided by nearby tested pairs — that selectivity is real and reasonably consistent (predominantly \(\sim\)1–20% for excitatory cortical pairs), but the number is governed by the denominator: widen the eligible set from a local neighbourhood to the whole brain and it falls by more than an order of magnitude. Local wiring is also structured, not merely sparse — neurons of like feature or cell type connect preferentially, beyond what physical proximity predicts — though the fly mushroom body shows that sparsity can be nearly random. Across species, this local selectivity does not track cognitive ability, which instead scales with the number of neurons.

1. The measure

A neuron's raw synapse count is a poor measure of selectivity — it mostly tracks brain size, not choosiness. A mouse cortical neuron carries several thousand synapses, yet contacts only a small, particular subset of the cells whose axons and dendrites pass close enough to reach it. The quantity that captures selectivity is the local connection probability — among the neurons close enough to be plausible partners, the fraction a neuron actually connects to:

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:phi} \phi \;=\; \frac{\#\,\text{connected pairs}}{\#\,\text{nearby tested pairs}}. \end{equation}\]

This is the right measure for three reasons. It is what paired-recording experiments measure directly, by patching two nearby neurons and testing for a synapse. It normalises out sheer neuron count — a cell with thousands of synapses can still have a low \(\phi\), whereas a synapse total mostly reflects brain size. And it is comparable across circuits once the denominator is fixed — which nearby pairs count as “plausible partners.” And because it is a ratio, the denominator dominates the number: widen it from the local neighbourhood to the whole brain and \(\phi\) drops by more than an order of magnitude — in the fly connectome, 71% of connections fall among the <3% of neuron pairs within 50 µm (Lin 2024), so a “fraction of all neurons” figure mostly reports brain size.

2. Selective, not dense

Across rodent and human neocortex, and across recording methods, the excitatory pyramidal-to-pyramidal \(\phi\) among nearby cells sits predominantly at \(\sim\)1–20%, with the bulk in the single digits. In rat layer 2/3 it falls from \(0.09\) to \(0.01\) as the distance between cell bodies grows from 25 to 200 µm (Holmgren 2003); a large mouse-and-human survey reports an overall \(7.3\%\) with peak recurrent excitation near \(5\%\) (Campagnola 2022); human layer 2/3 rises to the upper teens but stays under \(20\%\).

These values are lower bounds. Cutting a brain slice severs axons, so paired-recording under-counts real connectivity. Voltage imaging makes the bias plain: in rat hippocampal area CA3, optical recording found a \(5.3\%\) connection rate where same-condition patch-clamp found only \(0.47\%\), still below the \(14.9\%\) implied by anatomy (Fekete 2025). Correcting the undercount pushes \(\phi\) up, never out of the band.

The band is representative, not cherry-picked. The densest local excitatory circuits known — clustered layer-5 pyramidal subnetworks, or the barrel L4 spiny stellates at \(24.3\%\) (Lefort 2009) — either sit just above the band or are specific, named pathways. The one motif that is reliably dense is inhibition: a pyramidal cell and a neighbouring fast-spiking interneuron connect with probability \(0.5\)–\(0.75\). The honest summary is not “everything is sparse” but “excitatory recurrence is bounded, with a few cell-type-specific pathways dense by design.”

3. Structured, not merely sparse

Sparsity and structure are distinct: a neuron can sample few partners at random, or few partners selectively. The distinction is only decidable when feature-based targeting is measured after physical proximity is controlled — otherwise a “like connects to like” correlation can be a mere spatial-overlap artifact (the geometric baseline is Peters' rule).

Two connectomic studies clear that bar. Fusing electron-microscopy wiring with two-photon recordings of the same mouse visual-cortex neurons, Ding 2025 finds that after regressing out how far axons and dendrites travel together, only a neuron's response tuning — not its spatial position — predicts fine-scale connectivity. A millimetre-scale inhibitory census finds “widespread specificity, with many interneurons differentially targeting spatially intermingled subpopulations” (Schneider-Mizell 2024) — differential targeting of equally-available, same-depth cells is proximity control by construction. And at matched \(\lt\)100 µm, excitatory neurons contact fast-spiking interneurons at \(46\%\) versus other pyramidal cells at \(6\%\) (Oldenburg 2022).

The counterpoint: sparse need not mean structured. Each Kenyon cell of the fly mushroom body integrates about seven olfactory inputs drawn in an apparently random combination (Caron 2013) — sparsity carrying no identity structure at all. Sparse and structured are separate claims, and both are needed to describe cortex.

4. Selectivity and cognition

Local selectivity does not track cognitive ability across species. A targeted search finds no study relating a local-connectivity measure to cognition between species; what the comparative literature relates cognition to is scale: across 36 species, absolute brain volume best predicts self-control (MacLean 2014); across 111 birds, the number of neurons in the forebrain tracks behavioural innovation (Sol 2022). It also refutes the idea that cleverer species wire more sparsely: within a single study, method, and distance window, human layer 2/3 connectivity (\(\sim\)19%) exceeds mouse visual cortex (\(\sim\)5–10%) (Seeman 2018): whatever local selectivity buys, it is not a monotone dial on intelligence.

5. Conclusion

A neuron's selectivity has no single value; it exists only against a stated local opportunity set — nearby tested pairs, arbor-overlapping pairs, or potential synaptic sites, never the whole brain. Local sparsity is therefore a design constraint on cortical wiring, not a proxy for cognitive ability: across species, cognition scales with the number of neurons, not with how selectively each one wires.
