Local connection selectivity across species

A neuron connects to only a small, structured fraction of the partners it could reach nearby — on both the input and output side — and how selective it is varies from one cell type to the next and from one species to another.

Referencereferencereproduce: ed6da8a · master

Summary. A cortical neuron carries thousands of synapses yet connects to only a minority of the neurons it could reach locally — on both the input (fan-in) and output (fan-out) side. Measured as a local connection probability — connected pairs among nearby eligible pairs — that selectivity sits predominantly at \(\sim\)1–20% for excitatory cortical pairs, is structured rather than random (like-tuned and same-type cells connect preferentially, beyond what proximity predicts), and varies markedly — across cell types, from \(\sim\)5% excitatory recurrence to 50–75% for pyramidal–fast-spiking pairs, and across species, from \(\sim\)5–10% in mouse to the upper teens in human layer 2/3. Whether this local selectivity bears any relation to a species' cognitive ability is, as of this writing, simply unexamined: no study has entered a local wiring-selectivity measure into a cross-species cognitive comparison. What cognition does track is the number of neurons.

1. The measure

A neuron's raw synapse count is a poor measure of selectivity — it mostly tracks brain size, not choosiness. A mouse cortical neuron carries several thousand synapses, yet contacts only a small, particular subset of the cells whose axons and dendrites pass close enough to reach it. The quantity that captures selectivity is the local connection probability — among the neurons close enough to be plausible partners, the fraction a neuron actually connects to:

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:phi} \phi \;=\; \frac{\#\,\text{connected pairs}}{\#\,\text{nearby tested pairs}}. \end{equation}\]

Because it counts connected pairs, \(\phi\) captures selectivity on both sides at once: how few of its available partners a neuron receives from (its fan-in) and how few it sends to (its fan-out). It is what paired-recording experiments measure directly, by patching two nearby neurons and testing for a synapse, and it normalises out sheer neuron count — a cell with thousands of synapses can still have a low \(\phi\), whereas a synapse total mostly reflects brain size. The one thing to hold fixed is the denominator — which nearby pairs count as “plausible partners.” The fraction is defined against a local eligible set, not the whole brain, and is only comparable across circuits once that set is fixed; every number below uses nearby tested pairs.

2. Selective, and variable

Across rodent and human neocortex, and across recording methods, the excitatory pyramidal-to-pyramidal \(\phi\) among nearby cells sits predominantly at \(\sim\)1–20%, with the bulk in the single digits — a small minority of the locally available partners. But it is not one number: it varies with cell type and with species. In rat layer 2/3 it falls from \(0.09\) to \(0.01\) as the distance between cell bodies grows from 25 to 200 µm (Holmgren 2003); a large mouse-and-human survey reports an overall \(7.3\%\) with peak recurrent excitation near \(5\%\) (Campagnola 2022); and in one study, method, and distance window, human layer 2/3 (\(\sim\)19%) sits several-fold above mouse visual cortex (\(\sim\)5–10%) (Seeman 2018).

These values are lower bounds. Cutting a brain slice severs axons, so paired-recording under-counts real connectivity. Voltage imaging makes the bias plain: in rat hippocampal area CA3, optical recording found a \(5.3\%\) connection rate where same-condition patch-clamp found only \(0.47\%\), still below the \(14.9\%\) implied by anatomy (Fekete 2025). Correcting the undercount pushes \(\phi\) up, never out of the band.

The band is representative, not cherry-picked. The densest local excitatory circuits known — clustered layer-5 pyramidal subnetworks, or the barrel L4 spiny stellates at \(24.3\%\) (Lefort 2009) — either sit just above the band or are specific, named pathways. The one motif that is reliably dense is inhibition: a pyramidal cell and a neighbouring fast-spiking interneuron connect with probability \(0.5\)–\(0.75\). The honest summary is not “everything is sparse” but “excitatory recurrence is bounded, with a few cell-type-specific pathways dense by design.”

3. Structured, not merely sparse

Sparsity and structure are distinct: a neuron can sample few partners at random, or few partners selectively. The distinction is only decidable when feature-based targeting is measured after physical proximity is controlled — otherwise a “like connects to like” correlation can be a mere spatial-overlap artifact (the geometric baseline is Peters' rule).

Two connectomic studies clear that bar. Fusing electron-microscopy wiring with two-photon recordings of the same mouse visual-cortex neurons, Ding 2025 finds that after regressing out how far axons and dendrites travel together, only a neuron's response tuning — not its spatial position — predicts fine-scale connectivity. A millimetre-scale inhibitory census finds “widespread specificity, with many interneurons differentially targeting spatially intermingled subpopulations” (Schneider-Mizell 2024) — differential targeting of equally-available, same-depth cells is proximity control by construction. And at matched \(\lt\)100 µm, excitatory neurons contact fast-spiking interneurons at \(46\%\) versus other pyramidal cells at \(6\%\) (Oldenburg 2022). This targeting is an output-side selectivity — which cells an axon picks out among equally available partners — and it too differs by species: a dense-EM comparison of mouse and human cortex finds an expanded interneuron-to-interneuron network in human, driven by interneuron-targeting interneuron types with heightened synaptic selectivity for interneuron partners (Loomba 2022).

The counterpoint: sparse need not mean structured. Each Kenyon cell of the fly mushroom body integrates about seven olfactory inputs drawn in an apparently random combination (Caron 2013) — sparsity carrying no identity structure at all. Sparse and structured are separate claims, and both are needed to describe cortex.

4. Cognition: an open question

Does any of this bear on how cognitively able a species is? No one has checked. A targeted search across fifteen query variants — spanning PubMed, Semantic Scholar, and the primary literature — finds no study that enters a local wiring-selectivity measure (connection probability, fan-in, or filling fraction) into a cross-species cognitive comparison with the usual confound controls. The two literatures that would have to meet do not: microcircuit-connectivity studies measure local selectivity but stop at one to three species and never introduce a cognitive variable, while comparative-cognition studies regress cognition on global scale — absolute brain volume across 36 species (MacLean 2014), forebrain neuron number across 111 birds (Sol 2022) — but never on a local wiring measure. The one connectivity variable compared broadly, synapse density, is roughly invariant across primates, so it cannot be what cognition tracks. On present evidence cognition scales with the number of neurons, not with how selectively each one wires.

Nor do the stray fragments point the intuitive way. Were “cleverer species wire more sparsely” true, human local connectivity should undercut a mouse's; in the one matched comparison it does the opposite — human layer 2/3 exceeds mouse visual cortex (Seeman 2018) — but that is a by-product of a circuit-cartography study, not a test of the question. The honest position is not that local selectivity fails to predict cognition, but that the correlation has never been examined.

5. Conclusion

A neuron connects to only a small fraction of the partners available to it locally, on both the input and output side; that selectivity is structured rather than random, and it varies substantially — from a few per cent of excitatory recurrence to the majority of pyramidal–fast-spiking pairs, and from mouse to human. What it does not yet connect to is cognition: no study has related a species' local wiring selectivity to its cognitive ability, and what cross-species cognition tracks instead is the number of neurons. Whether local selectivity is merely a design constraint on wiring or also a lever on cognition is, for now, an open and unexamined question.
