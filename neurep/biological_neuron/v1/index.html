Local fan-in selectivity across species

How selectively neurons wire relative to what they could connect to locally — and whether it tracks cognition. reference

archived · v1 · 2026-07-04 · 2ae1df3 — view latest

Referencereferencereproduce: 7623818 · master

TL;DR. A neuron can carry thousands of synapses and still be highly selective relative to the partners it could reach locally. This node verifies, against primary sources, four claims about that local selectivity. (1) Local excitatory cortical circuits are selective, not dense: the pair-level connection probability sits predominantly at \(\sim\)1–20% (typically single-digit to mid-teens), with a few specific pathways denser still. (2) The number is governed by the denominator — re-denominating one system from a local neighbourhood to the whole brain drops the connection probability by more than an order of magnitude. (3) Local wiring is structured, not merely sparse, wherever it is measured after distance and density are controlled — but not universally (the fly mushroom body is sparse yet apparently random). (4) No primary study surfaced, under a pre-registered search, linking local selectivity to cross-species cognitive ability; comparative cognition instead tracks neuron counts and brain size. The upshot for artificial networks: bounded, structured, cell-type-specific local integration is a plausible scalability principle, not a standalone predictor of reasoning.

1. The denominator problem

Ask how many neurons a given neuron “listens to,” and the raw synaptic fan-in is the wrong answer: a mouse cortical neuron carries on the order of \(\sim\)8,200 synapses, yet it contacts a tiny, structured subset of the cells it could physically reach. The computationally interesting quantity is relative — connected partners divided by the partners that were geometrically and biologically eligible. Write the eligible presynaptic set of neuron \(i\) as \(E_{\mathrm{in}}(i)\) and the count of distinct presynaptic partners as \(d^{\mathrm{unique}}_{\mathrm{in}}(i)\). The pair-level local selectivity is

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:phipair} \phi^{\mathrm{pair}}_{\mathrm{in}}(i) \;=\; \frac{d^{\mathrm{unique}}_{\mathrm{in}}(i)}{\lvert E_{\mathrm{in}}(i)\rvert}, \qquad\text{population form}\quad P_{\mathrm{conn}}(A\!\to\! B \mid \mathrm{local}) \;=\; \frac{\#\,\text{connected tested pairs } A\to B}{\#\,\text{eligible/tested local pairs } A\to B}. \end{equation}\]

Everything turns on the choice of \(E_{\mathrm{in}}(i)\) — the denominator. It can be the whole brain, neurons within a radius, arbor-overlapping pairs, or, most finely, the axon–dendrite appositions close enough to form a synapse. That last, site-level definition gives the potential-synapse filling fraction

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:phisite} \phi^{\mathrm{site}} \;=\; \frac{\#\,\text{actual synapses}}{\#\,\text{potential synaptic sites (axon--dendrite appositions)}}, \end{equation}\]

which asks a categorically different question (how many microscopic opportunities are occupied) and must never be compared to a pair-level probability. A fourth, weight-aware notion — the effective fan-in, an inverse-participation-ratio count that discounts many tiny inputs — connects the biology to bounded-fan-in regularisation in artificial networks:

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:eff} d^{\mathrm{eff}}_{\mathrm{in}}(i) \;=\; \frac{\bigl(\sum_j w_j\bigr)^2}{\sum_j w_j^2},\qquad w_j \ge 0. \end{equation}\]

These denominators form a strictness ladder — whole-brain \(\prec\) radius/tested-pair \(\prec\) arbor-overlap \(\prec\) potential-synapse-site — and a single “neurons are \(X\%\) selective” headline is meaningless without saying which rung it sits on. Every quantity below is verified against a primary source (methods in §7).

2. Local excitatory connection probability

Across rodent and human neocortex, and across recording methods, the pair-level excitatory connection probability at the tested-pair rung sits predominantly at \(\sim\)1–20%, with the mode in the single digits. In rat layer 2/3 it falls from \(0.09\) to \(0.01\) as intersomatic distance grows from 25 to 200 µm; a large mouse+human multipatch census reports an overall \(1731/23620 = 7.3\%\) with peak recurrent E→E near \(5\%\); human L2/3 rises to the mid-teens but stays under \(20\%\).

The sparse picture is method-robust, not a slice artifact. Paired-recording in slices severs axons and therefore undercounts connectivity — so these values are lower bounds. Voltage imaging in rat CA3 makes the bias explicit: optical measurement recovered \(5.3\%\) where same-condition patch-clamp found only \(0.47\%\) (an \(\sim\)11× gap), still below the anatomical ceiling of \(14.9\%\). Correcting the undercount pushes values up, never out of the \(1\text{--}20\%\) band.

“Predominantly” is a representative claim, not a selected one. An active contradictor search (11 queries, six candidate dense circuits) surfaced exactly one genuine band-breaker: within-barrel L4→L4 spiny-stellate connectivity at \(24.3\%\) (corroborated by Feldmeyer 1999). The other candidates report in-band values or concern structure atop a sparse baseline — Song 2005's \(11.6\%\) is the archetype, Perin 2011's \(\sim\)21% appears only at extreme short range, and the named L5 thick-tufted “dense cluster” gives only \(5.2\%\)/\(3.2\%\). So the honest statement is interactional: local excitatory connectivity is bounded, with specific pathways (L4→L4 \(\sim\)24%; pyr↔fast-spiking interneurons \(0.5\text{--}0.75\)) locally dense by design.

3. Denominator dependence

Because \(\phi\) is a ratio, broadening \(E_{\mathrm{in}}(i)\) at fixed actual-connection count drives it down — the falsifiable content is the magnitude of that drop within a single system, not its (arithmetically guaranteed) direction. The Drosophila whole-brain connectome supplies a clean within-system rung: over all pairs the connection probability is \(P_{\mathrm{conn}} = 1.61\times10^{-4}\) (\(\sim\)0.016%), yet “over 71% of connections occur between neuron pairs within 50 µm of each other, despite these pairs constituting less than 3% of the total.” Re-denominating to that local, arbor-overlap neighbourhood gives an implied local rate of \(\sim\)0.38% — a \(\sim\)24× jump from the same connectome's own figures, comfortably past an order of magnitude, and a floor since “over 71%” and “less than 3%” bound the numerator and denominator the conservative way.

The potential-synapse filling fraction of \eqref{eq:phisite} lives on a different axis: in mouse neocortex \(\phi^{\mathrm{site}} = 0.26\), in rat hippocampus \(0.22\text{--}0.34\) — a reconstruction-based estimate, so unlike slice \(\phi^{\mathrm{pair}}\) it is not subject to the axon-severing undercount. That neurons occupy roughly a quarter to a third of their microscopic synaptic opportunities is “substantial but not dense,” and it answers a genuinely different question from the pair-level probabilities — which is exactly why the two must never share an axis.

4. Structure versus sparsity

Sparsity and structure are distinct: a neuron can sample few partners at random, or few partners selectively. The distinction matters for inductive bias, and it is decidable only when feature-based targeting is measured after the geometric opportunity (proximity, density) is controlled — otherwise a “like-to-like” correlation may be a spatial-overlap artifact.

Excitatory like-to-like survives a proximity control. Fusing MICrONS electron microscopy with two-photon imaging of the same mouse V1 neurons, Ding 2025 separates tuning similarity into feature and spatial components and regresses out the axon–dendrite co-travel distance (a Peters'-rule geometric null stronger than soma distance): only the feature component predicts fine-scale connections beyond what proximity explains, and connected pairs are more tuning-correlated than unconnected controls whose dendrites pass within 5 µm of the axon. Inhibitory targeting is cell-type-specific by design. A millimetre-scale connectomic census (Schneider-Mizell 2024; 1352 cells, \(\gt\)70,000 inhibitory synapses) shows “widespread specificity, with many interneurons exhibiting differential targeting of spatially intermingled subpopulations” — differential targeting of equally available, same-depth targets is distance control by construction, and it survives a null matched by depth bin, cell type, and compartment. Optogenetics adds a distance- and density-matched contrast: at \(\lt\)100 µm, excitatory neurons contact fast-spiking interneurons at \(46.3\%\) versus pyramidal cells at \(5.8\%\) (\(p \approx 10^{-9}\)); and human cortex shows an expanded interneuron-to-interneuron network relative to mouse.

The counterpoint: sparse need not mean structured. In the Drosophila mushroom body each Kenyon cell integrates on average \(\sim\)7 projection-neuron inputs (2–11 dendritic claws, \(n=200\)) in an apparently random combination — the canonical expansion-layer code for associative learning. That randomness is apparent, not strict: it carries a documented, modest neurogeometric bias (a strict “no-enrichment” framing did not survive adversarial verification), and later electron microscopy gives a slightly lower median. Still, single-Kenyon-cell input sets are sparse and non-stereotyped — sparsity here does not imply identity-structured targeting, which is precisely why sparsity and structure are separate claims.

5. Cross-species cognition

Does local fan-in selectivity track abstract-reasoning or learning ability across species? A pre-registered search — 15 query variants across PubMed/PMC, Semantic Scholar, and the Scholar-indexed literature, with ten near-miss papers logged — surfaced no primary study that regresses a local connection-selectivity measure on a cross-species cognitive measure with confound control. The verdict is scoped to the recorded protocol: no such study surfaced, not none exists. What the comparative literature does establish is a link to global scale: across 36 species, absolute brain volume best predicts self-control; across 111 bird species, pallial telencephalon neuron number tracks innovation propensity; and large-brained corvids and parrots reach primate-like forebrain neuron counts in far smaller brains. Cognition, as measured, tracks the size of the associative neuron population — not any local wiring-selectivity statistic.

This also settles a tempting but false hypothesis: that more intelligent species always wire more sparsely locally. In a single study — Seeman 2018, the 8-electrode multipatch dataset behind the mouse-V1 row above — human L2/L3 pyramidal connectivity within 100 µm (\(\sim\)18.8–18.9%) exceeds mouse V1 in the same window (\(\sim\)4.8–10%): one method, one distance cutoff (a distinct dataset from the pooled human \(15.8\%\) of Planert 2025). The “smarter \(\Rightarrow\) sparser” ordering is cleanly refuted by that matched comparison — not by the intake's original cross-study \(15.8\%\)-vs-\(7\%\) contrast, which confounds lab, method, and cutoff.

6. Synthesis

The load-bearing conclusion is not “everything is sparse.” It is that local biological wiring is bounded, structured, and cell-type-specific, that its measured “selectivity” is a property of the denominator as much as of the biology, and that this local selectivity is decoupled from cross-species cognition once population scale is accounted for. Cognitive ability plausibly depends on an interaction — associative neuron count, local bounded selectivity, cell-type diversity, plasticity, and long-range organisation — in which bounded local fan-in is a necessary scalability feature for building large computers out of neurons, not a sufficient explanation of reasoning. For a per-neuron representation prior, the biology endorses the form of the inductive bias — sample a bounded, structured, cell-type-aware subset of local inputs (the effective fan-in of \eqref{eq:eff}) — while cautioning that the structure, not the sparsity alone, is what carries function.

Verdict. On its pre-registered pass/fail rule the review passes: all four claims are supported by primary sources — sparsity (with named dense, above-band exceptions), the \(\sim\)24× within-system denominator drop, an unrefuted and provenance-recorded null on cognition, and distance-controlled structure — the “smarter \(\Rightarrow\) sparser” negative control is cleanly refuted, and traceability is 35/36 (32 confirmed, 3 corrected, 1 unverifiable). It is a synthesis reference node, not a verdict on a neurep method.

7. Verification

This node substitutes source verification for a compute campaign: no models, seeds, or cluster jobs. Its “experiment” is a deep-research harness that fans out web searches, fetches primary sources (journal / PMC / publisher — not blogs or trackers), extracts each quantity from an abstract, methods section, or named table, and subjects it to three-vote adversarial verification (a claim dies on \(\ge 2/3\) refutations). Every headline quantity was frozen into a tiered manifest before the searches ran, and each received one of three verdicts: confirmed (the source states the value), corrected (a materially different value or qualification, recorded with the original), or unverifiable (no accessible primary source). Denominator class, region, cell type, soma-distance, method, and evidence quality are tagged per record so that no two rungs of the strictness ladder are ever conflated; a separate contradictor search guards the representativeness of “predominantly” against selection bias.

Of 36 value-bearing quantities, 32 confirmed verbatim, three were corrected, and one (a claim that primate cortical neurons carry fewer synapses per neuron than mouse) could not be sourced and is reported as unverifiable. (i) The human \(15.8\%\) figure (Planert 2025) rests on a single Charité Berlin dataset; Peng 2024, cited by the intake as a second source, analyses the directed-acyclic structure of the identical recordings — one measurement, not two independent replications. (ii) The mushroom-body “random” convergence is apparently random with a documented weak neurogeometric bias. (iii) Tecuatl 2021's potential-connectivity estimates are for the rodent hippocampal formation, not mouse neocortex. One further data-label correction concerns a corroborating source (an all-distance total mislabelled as a within-100 µm subset), which leaves the conclusions untouched. The complete per-quantity ledger, including both search-provenance records, is the committed reproduce trail at results/claim_records.json.
