A shared reference for the studies' metrics

Intuition and formulae for the statistics, clustering, and structure metrics the reports share.

Referencereferencereproduce: 9a88b01 · feature/runstore-pipeline

TL;DR. The studies reuse one toolbox — a significance protocol, clustering-agreement scores, graph-modularity measures, subspace-geometry probes, variance-based sensitivity, and learning-curve summaries. This page defines each metric once: a one-line intuition, the formula as it is actually computed, its range, and which studies use it. Reports link here instead of re-deriving. It is reference material, not a hypothesis test — there is no verdict to read.

1. Conventions

Symbols used throughout. A layer's weight matrix is \(W\); its entrywise magnitude \(|W|\) is the data-free weight proxy some metrics price edges by. A neuron's learned per-neuron code (the representation a prior acts on) is \(\phi_i\), or \(\sigma_i\in[0,1]^K\) once squashed. A learning curve plots test \(\text{MSE}\) against training-set size \(n\) on a log axis. A seed is one independent run (fresh initialisation, and a fresh graph/data draw where a study says so); aggregates run over the seeds.

Several geometry metrics share one construction. To probe a target module \(k\), resample only the raw inputs it depends on (the exogenous coordinates in its receptive field, the rest held fixed), collect the resulting hidden-activation displacements \(\Delta H\) at the layer the module is read on, and take their top principal directions (by SVD) as an orthonormal basis \(U_k\in\mathbb{R}^{H\times q_k}\); the \(\lambda_i\) denote the singular/eigenvalues. Metrics that read \(U_k\) or \(\lambda_i\) are computed on this object. Across these studies a module is a disjoint group of raw input dimensions read at the first hidden layer, so "its inputs" are always network inputs. A genuinely intermediate module — fed by upstream hidden units rather than raw inputs — cannot have those units resampled directly without leaving the data manifold; intervene by activation patching a donor's real activations instead (the causal patching \(R^2\) of \eqref{eq:patch}).

Significance protocol (rule of the road for every paired comparison). Paper-grade comparisons use an equal hyperparameter budget, pick on validation and read test once, and at least five seeds. Significance is a paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test, Holm-corrected across a family of comparisons; uncertainty is a 95% bootstrap percentile interval over seeds (or \(\pm1\) s.e.m. on figures). These metrics are used across the studies — emergent_modularity, explicit_communication, collapse_investigation, axon_dendrite, and neurep_review — whose reports link back to this page. Where a metric has a canonical source it is cited inline; the study-specific probes are transcribed from the studies' own code.

2. Statistical inference & effect size

Paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test

Intuition. Asks whether a method's per-seed advantage over a baseline is consistently on one side of zero, without assuming the differences are Gaussian — it ranks the absolute paired differences and sums their signed ranks.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:wilcoxon} W_{+}=\sum_{i:\,d_i>0} R_i,\qquad d_i=x_i-y_i,\quad R_i=\operatorname{rank}(|d_i|),\qquad W=\min(W_{+},W_{-}), \end{equation}\]

summing the ranks of the positive differences (\(W_{-}\) is the negative counterpart, and the reported statistic is \(W=\min(W_{+},W_{-})\)); the \(p\)-value comes from the signed-rank null (two-sided). Range \(p\in[0,1]\); significant at \(p<0.05\) after Holm correction.  ·  Ref. Wilcoxon 1945.

Cohen's \(d\) / \(d_z\) — effect size

Intuition. How many standard deviations separate the two conditions; the paired \(d_z\) divides the mean difference by the spread of the differences, so it rewards a consistent gap.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:cohen} d_z=\frac{\bar d}{s_d}\ \ (\text{paired}),\qquad d=\frac{\bar x-\bar y}{s_{\text{pool}}},\ \ s_{\text{pool}}=\sqrt{\tfrac12\big(s_x^2+s_y^2\big)}. \end{equation}\]

(the pooled \(s_{\text{pool}}\) shown assumes equal \(n\) per condition). Range \(\mathbb{R}\); by convention \(|d|\approx0.2,0.5,0.8\) is small, medium, large.  ·  Ref. Cohen 1988; Lakens 2013 (for \(d_z\)).

Bootstrap CI & s.e.m.

Intuition. Quantify how much a summary would wobble under resampling — draw the seeds with replacement many times and take the spread of the recomputed statistic.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:boot} \text{CI}_{95}=\big[\,Q_{2.5},\,Q_{97.5}\,\big]\big(\{\hat\theta^{*}_b\}_{b=1}^{B}\big), \qquad \text{s.e.m.}=\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}, \end{equation}\]

typically \(B=2000\) resamples, the resampling unit being the seed. Range an interval; narrower means more precise.  ·  Ref. Efron 1979.

Holm–Bonferroni correction

Intuition. When many hypotheses are tested at once, raise the bar so the chance of any false positive stays controlled; step down from the smallest \(p\), relaxing the threshold as you go.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:holm} \tilde p_{(k)}=\min\!\Big(1,\ \max_{j\le k}\big[(m-j+1)\,p_{(j)}\big]\Big),\qquad p_{(1)}\le\cdots\le p_{(m)}, \end{equation}\]

rejecting while \(\tilde p_{(k)}\le\alpha\). Range same units as \(p\); uniformly more conservative than raw \(p\).  ·  Ref. Holm 1979.

Spearman \(\rho\)

Intuition. Monotonic association — does \(y\) rise with \(x\) by rank, even if the relationship is curved; it is Pearson correlation computed on the ranks.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:spearman} \rho=1-\frac{6\sum_i d_i^2}{n\,(n^2-1)},\qquad d_i=\operatorname{rank}(x_i)-\operatorname{rank}(y_i). \end{equation}\]

Range \([-1,1]\); \(\pm1\) is a perfectly monotone relation.  ·  Ref. Spearman 1904.

Linear regression — Pearson \(r\), OLS \(\beta\), \(R^2\)

Intuition. Fit the best line; the slope \(\beta\) is the marginal effect of a predictor, \(R^2\) the share of variance it explains, and Pearson \(r\) the signed single-predictor version of \(\sqrt{R^2}\).

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:ols} \hat\beta=(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y,\qquad R^2=1-\frac{\sum_i (y_i-\hat y_i)^2}{\sum_i (y_i-\bar y)^2},\qquad r=\frac{\operatorname{cov}(x,y)}{\sigma_x\,\sigma_y}. \end{equation}\]

Range \(R^2\le1\) (0 = predicts the mean; can go negative out of sample); \(r\in[-1,1]\).  ·  Ref. Pearson 1896 (correlation; OLS/\(R^2\) are textbook).

3. Clustering & partition agreement

ARI — Adjusted Rand Index

Intuition. Do two partitions agree on which items share a group, corrected so that a random labelling scores zero — it counts agreeing item-pairs, adjusted for chance.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:ari} \text{ARI}=\frac{\displaystyle\sum_{ij}\binom{n_{ij}}{2}-\Big[\sum_i\binom{a_i}{2}\sum_j\binom{b_j}{2}\Big]\Big/\binom{n}{2}} {\tfrac12\Big[\sum_i\binom{a_i}{2}+\sum_j\binom{b_j}{2}\Big]-\Big[\sum_i\binom{a_i}{2}\sum_j\binom{b_j}{2}\Big]\Big/\binom{n}{2}}, \end{equation}\]

where \(n_{ij}\) is the number of items in both cluster \(i\) and class \(j\), and \(a_i,b_j\) the row/column sums. Range \([-1,1]\); \(1\) perfect, \(0\) chance.  ·  Ref. Hubert & Arabie 1985.

NMI — Normalized Mutual Information

Intuition. How much knowing one partition tells you about the other, rescaled to \([0,1]\) by the partitions' entropies.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:nmi} \text{NMI}=\frac{I(U;V)}{\tfrac12\big(H(U)+H(V)\big)},\qquad I(U;V)=\sum_{i,j}p_{ij}\log\frac{p_{ij}}{p_i\,p_j}. \end{equation}\]

Range \([0,1]\); \(1\) identical, \(0\) independent.  ·  Ref. Vinh, Epps & Bailey 2010 (arithmetic-mean variant).

Silhouette

Intuition. Is each point closer to its own cluster than to the nearest other one — a per-point cohesion-versus-separation contrast, averaged.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:silhouette} s_i=\frac{b_i-a_i}{\max(a_i,b_i)},\qquad S=\frac1n\sum_i s_i, \end{equation}\]

with \(a_i\) the mean intra-cluster distance and \(b_i\) the lowest mean distance to another cluster. Range \([-1,1]\); \(>0.5\) is sharp structure.  ·  Ref. Rousseeuw 1987.

Community count \(k\)

Intuition. How many groups the detector returns — either fixed in advance (spectral clustering on the graph Laplacian, "known-\(k\)") or chosen to maximise modularity (Louvain, "choose-\(k\)").

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:commk} k=\big|\arg\max_{\mathcal C} Q(\mathcal C)\big|\ \ (\text{Louvain}),\qquad \text{or fixed } k \ \ (\text{spectral, known-}k), \end{equation}\]

with \(Q\) the modularity of \eqref{eq:newmanq}. Range integer \(\ge1\); read against the planted group count.  ·  Ref. von Luxburg 2007 (spectral); Blondel et al. 2008 (Louvain).

4. Network & weight-graph modularity

Newman modularity \(Q\)

Intuition. The fraction of edge weight that falls inside communities minus what you would expect if edges were rewired at random keeping each node's degree; positive means real block structure.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:newmanq} Q=\frac{1}{2m}\sum_{i,j}\Big(W_{ij}-\frac{k_i k_j}{2m}\Big)\,\delta(c_i,c_j),\qquad k_i=\sum_j W_{ij},\ \ m=\tfrac12\sum_{ij}W_{ij}, \end{equation}\]

communities found by greedy Clauset–Newman–Moore on the \(|W|\)-weighted neuron graph. Range \([-\tfrac12,1]\); \(>0.3\) is strong.  ·  Ref. Newman 2004 (weighted \(Q\)); Clauset, Newman & Moore 2004 (greedy).

Barber bipartite modularity \(Q_{\text{blk}}\)

Intuition. Newman's idea for a two-sided graph (here input units vs. hidden units): excess within-block weight over the degree-product null, for a bipartite incidence matrix.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:barber} Q_{\text{blk}}=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{h,j}\Big(A_{hj}-\frac{k_h\,d_j}{m}\Big)\,\delta(c_h,c_j),\qquad A=|W_0|,\ \ k_h=\sum_j A_{hj},\ \ d_j=\sum_h A_{hj},\ \ m=\sum_{hj}A_{hj}. \end{equation}\]

Range \([-1,1]\); \(>0\) means factor-aligned blocks.  ·  Ref. Barber 2007.

Average Precision / Top-\(K\) F1 — edge recovery

Intuition. Rank candidate edges by aggregated weight and ask how early the true edges appear: AP averages the precision at each true hit; with \(K=|E|\) the top-\(K\) cut gives a single F1 (precision and recall coincide there).

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:ap} \text{AP}=\frac{1}{|E|}\sum_{k:\,e_k\in E}\frac{\big|\{e_{k'}\in E:\ k'\le k\}\big|}{k},\qquad \text{F1}_{@|E|}=\frac{\big|\text{top-}|E|\cap E\big|}{|E|}, \end{equation}\]

edges scored by the contracted weight \(a(u\!\to\!v)=\sum_\ell\sum_{i\in v,\,j\in u}|W^{(\ell)}_{ij}|\); chance \(=|E|/|\text{candidates}|\). Range \([0,1]\); \(1\) perfect.  ·  Ref. Manning, Raghavan & Schütze 2008 (§8.4).

5. Subspace geometry

All four read the module basis \(U_k\) (or its singular values \(\lambda_i\)) from §1.

Subspace dimensionality \(q^{v}\)

Intuition. How many principal directions a module needs to capture a fraction \(v\) of its activation-displacement variance — its effective rank at level \(v\).

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:qv} q^{v}=\min\Big\{\,q:\ \frac{\sum_{i\le q}\lambda_i^2}{\sum_i\lambda_i^2}\ge v\,\Big\},\qquad v=0.90. \end{equation}\]

Range integer \([1,H]\); small means a low-rank module.

axis_mass — single-neuron alignment

Intuition. Is a module carried by individual neurons (axis-aligned) or spread across many (rotated)? Take, for each basis direction, the squared loading of its top neuron, and average.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:axismass} \text{axis\_mass}=\frac1K\sum_{k}\frac{1}{q_k}\sum_{c=1}^{q_k}\max_{h}\,U_k[h,c]^2. \end{equation}\]

Range \([0,1]\); \(1\) = one neuron per direction (neuron-aligned), \(\approx 1/H\) = fully distributed.

Subspace overlap — off-diagonal

Intuition. Do two modules live in the same hidden directions? The squared cosines of the principal angles between their subspaces, averaged over module pairs.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:overlap} \text{overlap}=\operatorname*{mean}_{a\neq b}\ \operatorname{mean}\!\big(\sigma(U_a^\top U_b)^2\big), \end{equation}\]

where \(\sigma(\cdot)\) are singular values (the cosines of principal angles). Range \([0,1]\); \(0\) orthogonal, \(1\) identical.  ·  Ref. Björck & Golub 1973 (principal angles).

Participation ratio (PR)

Intuition. The effective number of dimensions activity actually uses — high when variance is spread evenly across directions, low when a few dominate.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:pr} \text{PR}=\frac{\big(\sum_i\lambda_i\big)^2}{\sum_i\lambda_i^2},\qquad \lambda_i=\text{eigenvalues of the activation covariance}. \end{equation}\]

Range \([1,H]\); a compressed layer sits well below its width \(H\).  ·  Ref. Gao et al. 2017.

6. Functional decomposition & causal mediation

Sobol first-order index \(S_k\) & interaction mass \(F\)

Intuition. Variance-based sensitivity: \(S_k\) is the share of output variance that would remain if input block \(k\) were the only thing held fixed; whatever is left after all first-order terms is non-additive interaction mass \(F\).

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:sobol} S_k=\frac{\operatorname{Var}_{x_k}\!\big(\mathbb{E}[y\mid x_k]\big)}{\operatorname{Var}(y)} \ \approx\ \frac{\mathbb{E}\big[(f_B-\mu)(f_{A_B^{k}}-f_A)\big]}{V},\qquad F=1-\sum_k S_k, \end{equation}\]

by the Saltelli pick-freeze estimator (\(A,B\) independent input draws, \(A_B^{k}\) is \(A\) with block \(k\) replaced from \(B\); clamp \(S_k\ge0\), cap \(\sum_k S_k\le1\)). Range \(S_k\in[0,1]\), \(F\in[0,1]\) — \(0\) additive, \(\to1\) interaction-dominated.  ·  Ref. Sobol 2001 (indices); Saltelli et al. 2010 (estimator).

Causal patching \(R^2\) (with rotation-blind control)

Intuition. A causal test of a module subspace: replace only its directions with a donor's and measure how much of the teacher's output change you reproduce. A random same-rank subspace is the null — the signal is real only if it clears that control.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:patch} R^2=1-\frac{\operatorname{Var}\big(\Delta\hat y-\Delta y^\star\big)}{\operatorname{Var}(\Delta y^\star)},\qquad \Delta\hat y=f\big(H+\Delta H\,P_k\big)-f(H),\ \ P_k=U_kU_k^\top, \end{equation}\]

with \(\Delta y^\star\) the teacher's output change and the control swapping \(P_k\) for a random rank-\(q_k\) projector \(QQ^\top\). Range \((-\infty,1]\); meaningful only when it far exceeds the control.  ·  Ref. Vig et al. 2020; Meng et al. 2022.

IIA / DAS — interchange-intervention accuracy

Intuition. Treat a (possibly rotated) subspace as if it were an intermediate variable: patch it from a source input and check the network's readout takes the source's value. DAS learns the rotation that makes this work best.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:iia} \text{IIA}=\frac1N\sum_{p=1}^{N}\mathbf{1}\big[\operatorname{bin}(\hat g^{\text{patch}}_p)=\operatorname{bin}(g^{\text{src}}_p)\big], \end{equation}\]

over \(N\) interchange pairs, where \(\hat g^{\text{patch}}\) is the readout of the intermediate after the patch and DAS optimises a rotation \(R\) so that patching the leading dimensions of \(Rh\) transports it. Range \([0,1]\); chance \(=1/(\text{number of bins})\).  ·  Ref. Geiger et al. 2022 (IIA); Geiger et al. 2024 (DAS).

7. Sample efficiency & learning curves

MSE

Intuition. Average squared error — the base task loss, read on train / validation / test, and on a shifted evaluation set for extrapolation.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:mse} \text{MSE}=\frac1n\sum_i (\hat y_i-y_i)^2. \end{equation}\]

Range \([0,\infty)\); on standardised targets \(\text{MSE}=1\) is the predict-the-mean floor.

AULC — area under the learning curve

Intuition. One number for sample efficiency: the area under test \(\text{MSE}\) plotted against log training-set size; lower means more is learned from less data.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:aulc} \text{AULC}=\frac{\displaystyle\int \text{MSE}(n)\,\mathrm{d}\!\left(\log_{10} n\right)}{\log_{10} n_{\max}-\log_{10} n_{\min}} \qquad(\text{trapezoid; } \text{MSE}\ \text{clipped to } 1). \end{equation}\]

Range \([0,\infty)\), effectively \([0,1]\) after the clip; lower is better.

Decay exponent

Intuition. How fast error falls as data grows — the slope of log-error against log-\(n\) in the large-\(n\) tail; a steeper slope means faster learning.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:decay} \log_{10}\text{MSE}\approx c-\alpha\,\log_{10} n\ \ (n\ge256),\qquad \alpha_{\text{theory}}=\frac{2s}{2s+r}\ \text{(confined)}\ \text{vs}\ \frac{2s}{2s+d}\ \text{(unconfined)}, \end{equation}\]

with \(\alpha=-\text{slope}\), \(s\) the target's smoothness, \(r\) the fan-in and \(d\) the ambient dimension. Range \(\alpha>0\); larger is faster.  ·  Ref. Stone 1982 (minimax rate).

Generalization gap (ID vs. OOD)

Intuition. How much worse a model does on held-out structure than on seen structure — here, the learning-curve area on novel input-mode combinations minus that on seen ones; \(0\) means it composes perfectly.

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap} \text{gap}=\text{AULC}_{\text{OOD}}-\text{AULC}_{\text{ID}}. \end{equation}\]

Range \(\mathbb{R}\), typically \(\ge0\); smaller is better.

8. Aliases & cross-uses

One concept, several names. Because the metrics were authored across studies, a few wear different labels. axis_mass is sometimes written axis_align; the subspace overlap of \eqref{eq:overlap} appears as off_diag or sub_offdiag; the causal patching \(R^2\) of \eqref{eq:patch} is the same probe as "activation-patching \(R^2\)" / patch_r2, and it does double duty — a geometry read (does a subspace exist) and a causal read (does it carry the module's computation). The Sobol index \eqref{eq:sobol} surfaces both as the functional-mass signal \(F\) and as a fit gate ("does the model respond to the signal inputs at all"). Newman \(Q\) \eqref{eq:newmanq} and Barber \(Q_{\text{blk}}\) \eqref{eq:barber} are distinct measures — unipartite vs. bipartite — and should not be conflated despite both being called "modularity".

Each study's report defines any bespoke, single-use diagnostics (collapse-timing, orthant packing, code-census measures) in place; only the shared, standard metrics live here.
