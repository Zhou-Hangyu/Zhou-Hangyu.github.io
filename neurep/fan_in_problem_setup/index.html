Learning unknown compositional structure through local connectivity

Near-minimax sparse estimators exist for compositional targets; can a network learn their local connectivity and reach a fixed error with fewer samples?

Bounded fan-in: realizing the sample-efficiency separation by GDreferencebuilds on: Sample complexity of bounded-arity DAG classes and their fan-in-limited MLP realizationsreproduce: 15078bc · master

Summary. Layered compositional targets can be estimated at rates controlled by the local dimensions and smoothness of their components rather than by the ambient input dimension. Sparse-network empirical-risk minimization attains these rates up to logarithmic factors even when the active supports are latent, and other statistical procedures adapt to unknown structure. Separate work makes sparse connectivity trainable, infers fixed topologies from data, or exposes compositional circuits through pruning. The missing result is whether a neuron-local connectivity bias, learned jointly with the predictor, consistently reduces the samples needed across varied continuous compositional targets. This is the immediate empirical goal. The stronger goals are a fixed-class optimization-to-rate theorem and, ultimately, adaptive performance across structural classes.

1. Motivation and statistical benchmark

Many scientific and engineering targets are built from local mechanisms: each subsystem depends on a few variables, and the full response composes many such interactions. When labeled data are limited, a learner that exploits this structure can require far fewer samples than one designed for arbitrary dependence on all \(d\) inputs. Compositional nonparametric theory shows that such savings are statistically possible. The question here is whether a generic neural network can learn the useful local connectivity from supervised data rather than being given it.

Consider the nonparametric regression model \(Y_i=f_0(X_i)+\xi_i\), \(i=1,\dots,n\), where \(X_i\stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim}P_X\) on \([0,1]^d\) with density bounded above and below (\(0\lt c\le p_X\le C\lt\infty\)) and \(\xi_i\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2)\). Write \(\mathcal E(\widehat f,f_0)=\lVert\widehat f-f_0\rVert_{L^2(P_X)}^2\) for the integrated squared error at a fresh covariate — a random quantity, since \(\widehat f\) depends on the sample — and \(\mathcal R_n(\mathcal F)=\inf_{\widehat f}\sup_{f_0\in\mathcal F}\mathbb E_{f_0}\,\mathcal E(\widehat f,f_0)\) for the minimax expected risk over a class \(\mathcal F\).

The statistical benchmark and the proposed architectural bias use related but different notions of locality. The benchmark assumes that the difficult nonlinear components have small local dimensions and prescribed smoothness, while leaving their active supports unknown. The proposed method imposes a stricter neuron-level bias by encouraging each unit to use only a few predecessors. This bounded-fan-in bias is therefore a learnable surrogate for the statistical local dimension–smoothness structure, not an exact characterization of it: a smooth high-dimensional aggregation node need not determine the statistical rate.1 Bounded fan-in is distinct from coordinate sparsity, low-dimensional projections, bounded interaction order, and architectural locality, although these notions overlap in special cases.

For layered compositions the benchmark is explicit. Take \(f_0=g_q\circ\cdots\circ g_0\) with \(g_i:\mathbb R^{d_i}\to\mathbb R^{d_{i+1}}\) whose coordinate functions are \(t_i\)-variate with Hölder smoothness \(\beta_i\), under the dimensional condition \(t_i\le\min(d_0,\dots,d_{i-1})\). The minimax rate is governed by the effective smoothness \(\beta_i^\star=\beta_i\prod_{\ell\gt i}(\beta_\ell\wedge 1)\):

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:rate} \mathcal R_n\bigl(\mathcal G(q,\mathbf d,\mathbf t,\boldsymbol\beta,K)\bigr)\;\asymp\;\phi_n\;=\; \max_{i=0,\dots,q}\, n^{-\frac{2\beta_i^\star}{2\beta_i^\star+t_i}} . \end{equation}\]

In the homogeneous case \(\beta_i=p\ge 1\) at every level with \(\max_i t_i=k\), \eqref{eq:rate} reduces to \(n^{-2p/(2p+k)}\), against the ambient-dimension rate \(n^{-2p/(2p+d)}\) of a generic \(p\)-smooth function of all \(d\) coordinates. At the level of these exponents, replacing \(d\) by \(k\) removes a factor \(\varepsilon^{-(d-k)/p}\) from the samples needed for root-\(L^2\) error \(\varepsilon\), exponential in the excess dimension \(d-k\). The parallel discrete statement is developed in the theory companion, Sample complexity of bounded-arity DAG classes and their fan-in-limited MLP realizations.

Equation \eqref{eq:rate} is a fixed-\(d\) rate; it does not give uniform scaling as \(d\) grows, where support entropy, graph multiplicity, and optimization difficulty may instead enter the within-class rate. The dimension sweeps of §3 are therefore empirical tests motivated by \eqref{eq:rate}, not direct verifications of it.

The learner is support-agnostic: never given the target's supports, wiring, or coordinate assignments, though it may know upper bounds on the class parameters — the maximum fan-in \(k\), the graph size \(S\), the depth \(D_G\), the smoothnesses \(\boldsymbol\beta\), and the radius \(K\). This raises three targets — an immediate empirical one about sample scaling, a fixed-class optimization-to-rate theorem, and adaptation across a union of structural classes — made precise in §3.

2. What existing work establishes

2.1 Statistical benchmarks and adaptation

For specified layered compositional classes, the minimax benchmark is known. Schmidt-Hieber (2020), with the correction of Schmidt-Hieber and Vu (2024), shows that a suitably tuned sparse ReLU ERM attains the effective-smoothness rate in \eqref{eq:rate} up to logarithmic factors, even though the active supports are latent. Related work gives rates for hierarchical-interaction models (Bauer and Kohler, 2019) and approximation advantages for graph-matched deep networks (Mhaskar et al., 2016; Poggio et al., 2017), with an efficient agnostic-learning guarantee in the discrete two-layer case (Lee et al., 1996). Huang et al. (2026) provide the closest graph-level analogue through a critical-path excess-risk bound for leveled DAGs, but the corresponding general-DAG minimax benchmark remains open.

Unknown composition structure can also be handled statistically. Classical model-selection results (Juditsky et al., 2009; Goldenshluger and Lepski, 2009; Baraud and Birgé, 2014), Bayesian and PAC-Bayesian procedures (Finocchio and Schmidt-Hieber, 2023; Steffen and Trabs, 2022), sparse penalized estimators (Ohn and Kim, 2022), and FAST-NN (Fan and Gu, 2024) provide oracle or adaptive rates under different structural models. Some include implementable optimization or sampling procedures, but none proves that the implemented procedure reaches a rate-achieving solution in polynomial resources for unknown internal neuron-level connectivity.

2.2 Supplied, inferred, or pruned structure

When the relevant structure is supplied, several methods realize related gains: networks matched to a known hierarchy (Cagnetta et al., 2024; Ren et al., 2026; Tomasini and Wyart, 2024), imposed modular decompositions (Boopathy et al., 2025), easy-to-hard curricula (Wang et al., 2025), convolutional locality (Malach and Shalev-Shwartz, 2021; Favero et al., 2021), known invariances (Tahmasebi and Jegelka, 2023), and prescribed structured architectures (Kim et al., 2026). These results show that structural bias can improve learning, but each result uses target-aligned architecture or training-side information unavailable in our setting.

Lin et al. (2026) infer a topology from covariate dependencies, freeze it, and then train the network weights. Do et al. (2026) train MLPs and use iterative prune–retrain steps to expose compositional circuits. These results show that connectivity selection can reveal useful structure, but neither jointly learns neuron-level supports from predictive loss nor measures the samples needed to reach a fixed prediction error. Lin's setup also makes the target structure visible in \(P_X\), unlike the independent-input regime considered here.

2.3 Gradient structure learning in restricted families

First-order methods can discover structure in specialized settings: they recover low-dimensional projections (Damian et al., 2022; Mousavi-Hosseini et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024), sparse coordinate structure in parity-type problems (Barak et al., 2022; Abbe et al., 2023), and hierarchical representations under controlled continuous models (Allen-Zhu and Li, 2023; Dandi et al., 2025). These are genuine positive results, but each relies on a particular target family, input distribution, or training protocol; none provides one neuron-local connectivity mechanism that transfers across varied continuous compositional targets.

2.4 Trainable sparsity

Sparse incoming connectivity is already trainable through dynamic sparse training (Lasby et al., 2024), fan-in regularization (Philipp and Carbonell, 2017), stochastic gates (Louizos et al., 2018), and pruning. Related theory gives generalization guarantees for compositionally sparse networks (Galanti et al., 2023) and local recovery guarantees for shallow sparse networks (Oymak, 2018; Fridovich-Keil and Pilanci, 2025). These results establish sparsification and restricted recovery, but not that learned internal connectivity produces compositional sample-scaling gains.

3. The gap and its evaluation

The immediate question is empirical: does jointly learned neuron-local connectivity reduce the samples needed to reach a fixed prediction error? Two theoretical questions follow: can a first-order learner match the sparse-ERM rate for one fixed class, and can one learner adapt across structural classes? Broad compositional classes can be computationally hard (Danhofer et al., 2025), so either theorem may require additional assumptions on the component functions, data distribution, signal strength, or information flow across levels.

3.1 Empirical success criterion

Evaluate the method by the number of samples required to reach a fixed test error, with active parameter count and optimization budget matched across methods. Results should hold across multiple support assignments, graph topologies, and component functions. Baselines should include a dense MLP, global sparsity, random constant fan-in, dynamic sparse training, a data-inferred fixed topology, and oracle wiring.

3.2 Two scaling regimes

Use two scaling regimes. In an irrelevant-coordinate sweep, hold the target graph fixed and append noise variables; this isolates input-support selection, where input-selection baselines such as LassoNet (Lemhadri et al., 2021) are the comparators. In an extensive compositional sweep, keep local arity and component regularity fixed while increasing graph size and depth so that all inputs remain relevant; this tests multilevel composition. Success in the second regime means slower growth in sample requirements than dense and globally sparse baselines, not constant sample complexity. Independent inputs prevent the structure from being inferred from \(P_X\) alone.

3.3 Theoretical endpoints

For a fixed class \(\eta\), the first theoretical target is a first-order method whose prediction risk matches the sparse-ERM rate up to logarithmic and optimization terms, under an explicit computational bound. The adaptive target asks one learner to compete with the best class in a union, paying a cross-class adaptation penalty. Equation \eqref{eq:oracle} states this stronger target: over a union \(\mathcal F_{\mathrm{union}}\) of layered classes, each indexed by class parameters \(\eta=(q,\mathbf d,\mathbf t,\boldsymbol\beta,K)\) with \(\mathcal F_\eta\) the union over its admissible active supports, one seeks a single first-order learner \(\widehat f_{\mathcal A}\), never given \(\eta\) or the supports, with

\[\begin{equation}\label{eq:oracle} \begin{aligned} &\sup_{f_0\in\mathcal F_{\mathrm{union}}}\Pr_{f_0}\!\Bigg[\; \mathcal E(\widehat f_{\mathcal A},f_0)\;\gt\;\\ &\qquad C\inf_{\eta\,:\,f_0\in\mathcal F_\eta} \Big\{\, r_n(\eta)+\operatorname{pen}_n(\eta,\delta)\,\Big\}\,\log^{c} n\;+\; C_{\mathrm{opt}}\,\varepsilon_{\mathrm{opt}}\;\Bigg]\;\le\;\delta , \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

for stated constants \(c, C, C_{\mathrm{opt}}\), where \(r_n(\eta)\) is the within-class rate, \(\operatorname{pen}_n(\eta,\delta)\) the cross-class adaptation penalty, and \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{opt}}\) the optimization tolerance. The optimization term must be tied to a stated empirical or penalized-objective gap, and the theorem must show how that gap affects prediction risk.

Compositional statistical rates and trainable sparse connectivity are known separately, and restricted methods can infer or expose useful compositional structure. The open question is whether one jointly trained neuron-local mechanism converts that structure into consistent sample savings across varied continuous targets. This empirical result is the immediate goal; optimization-to-rate theory is the longer-term endpoint.
